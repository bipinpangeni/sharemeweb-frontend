What Exactly Is Video Compression and Why Does It Look So Bad?

When an app compresses your video, it is essentially throwing away information. Video compression works by finding similar areas in consecutive frames and replacing them with a shorthand description rather than storing the full pixel data. At moderate compression this is nearly invisible. At the aggressive levels used by messaging apps — where they might reduce a 200 MB video to 8 MB — the quality loss becomes very obvious. Fine detail becomes blocky, motion looks smeared, and colours lose their accuracy.

The technical term for this is lossy compression, and once the quality is gone, it cannot be recovered. If someone downloads the compressed version and tries to re-edit it, the result will look even worse. This is a real problem for anyone sharing footage they plan to use professionally — wedding videos, event recordings, product demonstrations, real estate walkthroughs. Sending via WhatsApp and hoping for the best simply is not an option if quality matters.

What About YouTube's "Unlisted" Option?

Some people use YouTube's unlisted video feature as a workaround for private video sharing — you upload the video, set it to unlisted, and share the link with specific people. This actually works reasonably well for many situations and YouTube does not compress video as aggressively as messaging apps. But there are some meaningful drawbacks worth considering.

First, unlisted on YouTube is not truly private. The link can be shared, the video can be found if someone has the URL, and YouTube has recently tightened its policies around unlisted videos. Second, YouTube re-encodes all uploaded video, which means you are not sharing the original file — you are sharing YouTube's processed version. For most casual uses this is fine, but for professional footage delivery it is not ideal. Third, YouTube requires you to have a Google account and the recipient needs a decent connection to stream at high quality — they cannot download the original file directly.

For private, full-quality video file delivery, Vault is simply the cleaner option. The recipient gets the actual file, not a stream.
