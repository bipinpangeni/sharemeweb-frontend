Individual Service Reviews

WeTransfer 7/10

WeTransfer is probably the most well-known file transfer service in the world, and for good reason — it was one of the first to make the experience genuinely simple. You upload, enter an email address, and the recipient gets a download link. No fuss. The 2 GB free limit is decent for most casual transfers, and the interface is clean and pleasant to use.

The weaknesses are mainly on the security side. Password protection, custom expiry times, and download limits are all paid features — you have to subscribe to WeTransfer Pro to access them. The free tier also requires you to enter an email address to send the transfer, which means WeTransfer collects recipient email data. If you just want to hand someone a file without involving email addresses, it is less convenient than a code-based system. For simple, no-frills transfers under 2 GB where security is not a concern, WeTransfer is a perfectly solid choice.

SendGB 7.5/10

SendGB has one of the most generous free file size limits in this category — 5 GB without an account. That alone makes it worth knowing about for very large file transfers. The interface is straightforward, and files persist for a surprisingly long 90 days on the free tier, which is useful if you need files to remain accessible for an extended period.

However, it falls short on security features. Password protection is a paid-only feature, there is no download limit control on the free plan, and the 90-day persistence — while generous in one sense — also means your files sit on their servers for a long time whether you want them to or not. If you are sharing sensitive content and want it cleaned up quickly after download, the lack of short expiry options is a real limitation. For large non-sensitive transfers where longevity is needed, SendGB is worth considering.

Filemail 6.5/10

Filemail is a solid professional-grade service that has been around for a while. The 5 GB free limit is generous, and it supports a desktop app for faster large file transfers. It works well for business use cases where you need reliable delivery to email recipients.

The main drawbacks for casual users are the email-based workflow — you have to enter recipient email addresses rather than just generating a shareable link — and the lack of security features on the free plan. Like most competitors, password protection is a paid feature. The 7-day expiry is fixed, and you cannot set a shorter window or a download limit. For professional users who primarily share via email and want a clean, reliable service, Filemail is a good option. For casual private sharing, it feels unnecessarily complicated.

Smash 6/10

Smash markets itself as a creative-focused file transfer service, and its branding is definitely slick and appealing. It handles file transfers of up to 2 GB on the free plan and does not require an account. The preview feature — which lets recipients preview some file types before downloading — is a nice touch.

The 2 GB limit is on the lower end for a free service in 2025, and — again — password protection and custom transfer options are paid. The 14-day expiry is fixed with no ability to shorten it. There is also a noticeable push to upgrade throughout the free experience, which some users find intrusive. For creative professionals who want a stylish delivery experience and are happy to pay for it, Smash's paid tier is worth a look. On the free plan, it does not stand out enough to compete with more generous alternatives.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is WeTransfer still worth using in 2025?

For simple, quick transfers under 2 GB where you do not need security features, WeTransfer is still a perfectly good service. It is reliable, well-known, and the recipient experience is smooth. It just does not offer the security controls or code-based flexibility that some use cases require.

What is the best free file transfer site for large files?

If raw file size is the priority and security is not a concern, SendGB offers 5 GB free without an account. If you need a large limit combined with security features, ShareMeWeb Vault's 3 GB with password protection and download limits is the best all-around free option.

Do any free file transfer services offer end-to-end encryption?

True end-to-end encryption — where not even the service provider can read your files — is rare on free plans. For files requiring that level of security, look at tools specifically designed for encrypted file transfer. For most everyday professional and personal file sharing, the combination of a unique code, password protection, and short expiry provides practical security that is more than adequate.

Why do some services require an email address?

Email-based delivery is a business decision as much as a feature choice. When you enter a recipient's email address, the service collects that data — potentially for marketing, for their own growth metrics, and to send follow-up communications. A code-based system like Vault requires no email input from either party, which is cleaner from both a privacy and a simplicity standpoint.
